# Move 2: The Hypothesis

Concept under test: interface (Cohort 7 Lectures 03 and 05)
Committed before any code. Timestamp = first commit in public repo.

## The Claim

A 100x learner who passes a normal "did you understand" check, meaning they can 
name "interface" and give an example, will still fail to derive it from first 
principles under one sharp follow-up (why must it exist, or what is the agreement 
actually made of), more often than not. And when the exact point where their 
derivation breaks is named for them, without being taught the answer, they can 
close that gap themselves more often than they stay stuck.

This holds two bets, tested separately in Move 5:

- Bet A, the gap is real and detectable: learners who recognize "interface" 
  cannot, unprompted, derive it under one follow-up.
- Bet B, naming the gap enables a self-close: pointing precisely at the break, 
  then staying silent, lets the learner generate a new, correct derivation in 
  their own words, unaided.

## Grounding (Move 1, three learners, all confirmed they took the lectures)

- All 3 recognized the term and produced an example instantly. (Sid: stove, 
  switchboard, shoelaces. Amrut: food-delivery app. Kartiken: this Zoom call.)
- All 3 failed to derive the full concept under one unprompted follow-up.
- The wall sat at different depths. Sid and Amrut held a surface or object view 
  and stalled at why interface exists (Amrut, verbatim: "that I'll have to think 
  about it"). Kartiken held the gestalt, connection between two parties, and even 
  spotted under-specification unprompted, but stalled at what the agreement is 
  made of (verbatim: "other than that, I don't know which one").
- Limitation, recorded honestly: in all three sessions the interviewer coached 
  toward the answer (fed the four pillars and worked the examples) instead of 
  naming the gap and waiting. So Bet A is well-evidenced (3 of 3), but Bet B has 
  not yet been cleanly observed. No learner has been given a precise gap-name and 
  silence and watched to see if they close it unaided. Move 5 exists to test 
  exactly this.

## Result that proves me right

Both Move 5 learners fail the unprompted derivation (Bet A holds), AND at least 
one produces a new, self-generated derivation after I name the gap without 
supplying the answer (Bet B holds).
(Grounded: 3 of 3 failed unprompted in Move 1, so a majority failing in Move 5 
is the expected, supported outcome.)

## Result that means no problem exists

Both learners derive interface cleanly under the first follow-up, with no help. 
If recognition and derivation do not split apart, the tool solves a problem 
nobody has.

## Kill-number (the line where I write "my hypothesis was wrong")

If, across the Move 5 sessions, naming the gap precisely and staying silent 
closes it for neither learner, meaning zero of two produce a self-generated 
derivation and any apparent "close" only happens after I supply the answer, then 
Bet B is false: the tool detects a gap it cannot help close, a diagnosis with no 
cure. At that result I stop defending the idea and record the hypothesis as wrong.

This kill-number sits on Bet B, not Bet A, on purpose. Bet A (the gap exists) is 
already strongly supported by 3 of 3 in Move 1, so a kill-number there is one I 
know cannot trigger, a cheat. Bet B is genuinely untested, and my own coaching 
reflex in all three Move 1 sessions is direct evidence that producing a real 
self-close, rather than a taught fake-close, may be hard. This line can actually 
be crossed. That is what makes it honest.

## Locked definitions (so I cannot grade generously later)

- Knows or recognizes interface: can name it and give at least one example.
- Fails to derive: under one open follow-up, cannot reconstruct why it must exist 
  or what the agreement is made of in their own words. Stalls, repeats a label, 
  or reaches for a memorized phrase.
- Real close (counts): a new, correct sentence in their own words that they had 
  not said before, produced after the gap is named and before any answer is 
  supplied.
- Fake close (does not count): nods, agrees, repeats the interviewer's words 
  back, or only derives after being told the answer.
